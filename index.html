1. Data Foundations

The reliability of any quantitative simulation is dictated by the quality and continuity of its underlying data. This platform utilizes the "Standard Epoch" approach to ensure 100% mathematical integrity across all instruments.

Historical Continuity (The 2002 Decision)

The simulation begins on July 1st, 2002. This is not arbitrary; it is the inception date of the PCRIX (PIMCO Commodity Real Return Strategy), which serves as our high-fidelity proxy for the Bloomberg Commodity Index (DJP).

Why not 1993? While the underlying S&P 500 total return data (SPY) launched in 1993, adding Commodities to the mix requires overlapping data. By anchoring to the 2002 threshold, we eliminate the risk of mismatched correlations and statistical extrapolation error in the early years of the backtest.

Dividend Accounting & Total Return

We utilize Adjusted Close prices fetched via the yfinance API. This methodology is critical for professional-grade backtesting because it ensures that all dividends, stock splits, and capital gains distributions are automatically "baked in" to the equity curve. The results represent Total Return (DRIP), assuming immediate reinvestment.

2. Synthetic Asset Synthesis

Since 3x and 4x leveraged ETFs often suffer from high expense ratios, tracking errors, and late inception dates, we synthesize their returns daily using a rigorous Cost of Carry model.

R_{synthetic} = (R_{underlying} × L) - (R_{financing} × (L - 1))
  • R_lev: The daily return of the leveraged synthetic asset.
  • L: The leverage factor (2.0 for VOO-2x, 4.0 for VOO-4x).
  • R_fin: The daily financing rate, proxied by VFISX (Short-Term Treasuries).

Example (4x VOO): If the S&P 500 rises 1% and the treasury rate is 0.01%, the synthetic 4x return is calculated as (0.01 * 4) - (0.0001 * 3) = 3.97%. This ensures our backtests account for the real-world cost of borrowing capital to achieved elevated exposure.

The Proof: Why Borrowing Costs Matter

A common question is: "Why is the formula for VOO (2x) 2x VOO minus 1x BILL?". To understand this, we must look at the balance sheet of a leveraged trade.

Imagine you have $10,000 and you want 2x leverage. To achieve this, you must:

  1. Use your $10,000 to buy VOO.
  2. Borrow another $10,000 in cash.
  3. Use that borrowed $10,000 to buy more VOO.
Your Position:
• Assets: $20,000 worth of VOO (200% exposure)
• Liabilities: $10,000 loan (100% financing)

At the end of the day, your return is the profit from your Assets minus the interest paid on your Liabilities. In our simulation, we use BILL (VFISX) as the proxy for that interest rate.

Net Return = (200\% \times R_{voo}) - (100\% \times R_{bill}) = 2R_{voo} - 1R_{bill}

For 4x VOO, the logic scales accordingly. To reach 400% exposure, you must borrow 300% of your capital's value, which leads to the formula: 4R - 3Rf.

3. Volatility Drag (Beta Slippage)

Leveraged instruments reset their exposure daily. In non-trending, volatile markets, this daily compounding leads to a "mathematical bleed" known as volatility drag or beta slippage.

The 3-Day Breakdown

Day VOO (1x) VOO (4x) Result
Day 0 $100.00 $100.00 Baseline
Day 1 $90.00 (-10%) $60.00 (-40%) Crash
Day 2 $100.00 (+11.1%) $86.66 (+44.4%) Recovery Deficit
Even though VOO returned to its original price, the 4x leveraged asset is still down 13.34%. This is why "Blind" leverage is a recipe for long-term failure. Our strategy is designed specifically to solve this by de-risking during high-volatility regimes.

4. Regime Detection (The SMA Shield)

We use the 200-day Simple Moving Average (SMA) as a Regime Filter, not a market-timing signal. Its purpose is to differentiate between high-probability growth regimes and structural bear markets.

Operational Logic

  • 🟢 Bullish Growth: When price is ≥ SMA 200, the "Search for Alpha" is active. The strategy follows the standard drawdown-based leverage scaling.
  • 🔴 Structural Protection: When price is < SMA 200, the strategy forces a "Safe State." Depending on your Quant Lab settings, the portfolio defaults to Tier 0 (Benchmark) or 100% Cash (BILLS).

This avoids the "slow bleed" of 2x-4x leverage during long-duration structural crashes like the 2000 Dot-com bubble or 2008 Financial Crisis.

5. The Hysteresis Engine (Tiers)

The strategy categorizes market "pain" into five distinct tiers, based on the percentage drawdown from the absolute All-Time High (ATH).

Tier Condition Strategy Intent
T0 Drawdown 0% - 5% Capital Preservation & Core Tracking
T1 Drawdown 5% - 10% Defensive Accumulation
T2 Drawdown 10% - 20% Neutral Positioning
T3 Drawdown 20% - 30% Aggressive Recovery Scaling
T4 Drawdown > 30% Maximum Alpha Tilt (Deep Recovery)

Hysteresis (Ratchet Logic)

Under Ratchet Logic, transitions are one-way during a crash. If the market hits T4, the strategy "locks in" that leverage level and refuses to de-escalate until a New All-Time High is achieved. This ensures the portfolio is fully tilted for the entire duration of the recovery rally.

6. Statistical Glossary

CAGR Compounded Annual Growth Rate. Calculated via geometric mean. It represents the "smooth" annual return required to reach the final value.
Sortino Ratio Measures return per unit of bad (downside) volatility. Unlike Sharpe, it doesn't penalize huge upward spikes (positive volatility), which is essential for alpha-seeking strategies.
Recovery Factor (Total Return / Max Drawdown). A measure of how efficiently the strategy "buys back" its losses. A value > 1.0 is generally considered robust.
Real Return Purchasing power growth adjusted for inflation using interpolated FRED CPI data.

7. System Architecture

The Strategy Tournament Laboratory is built on a high-performance, client-side simulation engine. It utilizes Float32Array memory optimization to ensure ultra-low latency.

Privacy First: All calculations for your custom "Quant Lab" strategies are performed locally in your browser. No financial parameters, weights, or boundaries are ever transmitted to our servers.

8. Ladder Philosophy

The core philosophy of this platform is the "Leverage Ladder". We believe that leverage should not be a static choice, but a dynamic reward earned by the market's stability.

By scaling exposure up as the market crashes, we are effectively rebalancing into higher future expected returns. By scaling exposure down as the market hits new highs, we "take chips off the table" and prepare for the next cycle of volatility.

9. Benchmarking Theory

We provide static benchmarks (1x, 2x, 4x) for the S&P 500 to serve as "Stress Tests."

  • 1x Benchmark: The baseline performance of a low-cost S&P 500 index fund.
  • 2x / 4x Benchmarks: Theoretical models that do not use our state-machine logic. These often show massive "terminal wealth" but suffer from drawdowns exceeding 90%, which are psychologically impossible for most human investors to hold through.

10. Risks & Limits

Every backtest has limitations. Professional investors must account for the following "real-world" frictions:

  • Slippage: The 0.01% - 0.10% cost of the bid-ask spread during rebalancing.
  • Gap-Down Risk: Our simulation uses daily close data. A "Flash Crash" that occurs mid-session or an overnight gap could exceed our drawdown bounds before a state transition is possible.
  • Tax Drag: In a taxable account, every transition between T1 and T4 would trigger a capital gains event. These strategies are best suited for tax-advantaged accounts (IRA / 401k).
